Researching apples and oranges

How user research changed our roadmap

Problem

I was working for a greengrocer called Green Apples that sold apples.

They wanted to reach the small retailers among whom oranges were a success.

They had no customer contacts and did not know what they liked about oranges - was it the taste, size, or color?

Green Apples invested in creating a small farm and started selling oranges to get ahead.

Yet the farm had no pesticides so the production had a bug infestation problem.

At the time of the research team was made of me and the Product Owner.

Process

To begin we defined an Opportunity Solution Tree to help us:

  • define the project goals,
  • find the goals opportunities (in other words problems or improvements),
  • invent experiments to achieve those goals.

We interviewed stakeholders, researched documentation and the current company’s OKR.

I created a survey to get a better understanding of the problem.

Although we should avoid open-ended questions on surveys, I decided to take the risk.

I wanted to see if there was any opportunity we may have missed in our initial research.

Also, I added a non-mandatory field to obtain the participants contact data.

Still, the written responses were not enough to understand the full context and motivation.

With the contacts I gathered I started doing interviews to dig in the survey’s results.

During all the process I worked on an Affinity Map.

I started by clustering the notes into groups we assumed existed based on the Opportunity Solution Tree.

With the survey’s answers and interview notes I tried to fill in the groups with more items.

Eventually, some groups disappeared and new ones showed up.

At the end the conclusions were clear.

Outcome

The research proved that the solution we had was heading in the right direction.

Furthermore, I discovered that we had some missing opportunities that affected our roadmap.

  • Users did not mind most bugs, only the crickets were a problem. We kept the farm and invested in cricket pesticide.
  • Users wanted oranges but they also wanted orange juice. We updated our discovery timeline to do additional research on the subject.

Learnings

At the beginning I did a lot of data analysis.

I had a lot of pressure to provide the right direction and management always loves numbers.

Sadly, the analysis was getting nowhere no matter how you queried the data.

Next time, I will negotiate with management to postpone the data analysis or time box it.
